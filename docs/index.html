See what a vision-language model sees.

CLIP maps images and sentences into the same 512-dimensional space, so “how similar are this photo and this phrase?” is a single dot product. Every demo below runs live in your browser — nothing you type or upload leaves your machine.

Model: openai/clip-vit-base-patch32, int8-quantized via transformers.js. Loads on first use (~65 MB text · ~90 MB vision), cached by your browser after that.

01 · The idea

One space, two encoders — trained so the diagonal lights up

Every gallery image, scored against every stored caption — straight from the database. Darker = more similar. The bright diagonal is the training objective.

How this works
CLIP was trained contrastively on 400 million (image, caption) pairs: pull each image toward its own caption, push it away from every other. This matrix needs no model download — the vectors were computed at ingest time and shipped with the page, so each cell is just one dot product between stored numbers.
Hover or tap any cell — rows are images, columns are their captions.
less similarmore similar
Why are the scores so small?
Raw CLIP cosines live in a narrow band — matching pairs land around 0.2–0.35. That is why the model ships a learned temperature of ~100 to sharpen them before any softmax; the Lab’s multi-label meters use it.

03 · The database

Fourteen images, embedded and auto-tagged at ingest

Click any image to open it in the Lab and inspect its numbers.

What each row stores
Every row in gallery.sqlite keeps a path, a caption, meta tags, and three float32 vectors: image_emb (512 — what it looks like), text_emb (512 — what its caption means), and fused_emb (1024 — both, concatenated and renormalized).

04 · The embedding space

All 512 dimensions, squashed onto your screen

Images that look alike land close together. Hover for an image’s true neighbours; click to open it in the Lab.

What am I looking at?
PCA squashes each 512-d embedding onto the two directions of most variation — the axes are not nameable concepts, and distances here only approximate distances in 512-d. The hover lines show each image’s 3 nearest neighbours in the full space, so you can see what the flattening distorts. Upload an image in the Lab and it lands on this map too.

05 · Search

Type a sentence, rank the database

Your query becomes a vector; every stored image is one dot product away.

average of visual + semantic similarity — one vector, both signals

score(item) = query_emb · item vector · under each result: looks = q·image_emb, means = q·text_emb — fused is exactly their average

The CLIP text encoder loads on your first search (~65 MB, cached after that).

06 · The Lab

Pick an image. Watch it become data.

Choose a gallery image or upload your own — then watch it become data.

Multi-class: top-5 tags

argsort the scores, keep 5 — every image gets exactly five, no matter what’s in it (tagger.py)

Multi-label: every tag decides for itself

per-tag sigmoid against the neutral prompt “a photo” — the label set is sized by the image (labels.py)

threshold 0.50

The embedding agent: propose ⇄ critique

A proposer drafts labels through one prompt template; a critic checks the work; only a passing record is published.

What the critic checks
Two dot products a human reviewer would approve of: aligned — does the caption’s embedding point back at the image? (≥ 0.20) — and confident — are the accepted labels sure of themselves, on average? (≥ 0.60). Fail with every template in the pool and the best draft stays unpublished (agent.py · item_tower.py).

07 · Recommend

Tap what you like. The two towers do the rest.

Tap a few hearts. Recommendations rank instantly — no model, just dot products against embeddings computed offline.

How two towers work
A two-tower recommender scores (user, item) pairs with one dot product between two encoders that share a space. The item tower is already finished — it’s the embeddings this page loaded. The user tower’s only job is to turn a user into one vector in that same space, and the simplest honest version is the renormalized mean of their liked items’ vectors (user_tower.py). Swap in a trained user model later; nothing else changes.

user_vec = unit( mean( [image ; text]/√2 of your likes ) ) · score(item) = item_emb · user_vec = looks + means

Tap two or three images you like — recommendations appear instantly.

08 · Optimize it

Same model, same weights, better answers

Everything above uses one checkpoint. These four levers — all implemented in the Python pipeline — make it more accurate or much faster without training anything.

Prompt ensembling

Embed each tag through many phrasings — “a photo of…”, “a drawing of…”, “a close-up of…” — and average the unit vectors. Phrasing noise cancels; meaning stays. ensemble.py

+3.5% zero-shot accuracy (CLIP paper, 80 templates)
Batching

One forward pass per tower per chunk of images, instead of two round-trips per image. The encoders are happiest when fed. features.extract_batch

~an order of magnitude on GPU ingest
Quantization

This very page runs the checkpoint in int8 (q8) — rankings barely move, downloads and memory shrink ~4×. The full-precision model stays offline.

4× smaller, near-identical rankings
Precompute the item side

Embeddings never change after ingest — so compute them offline, verify them with the agent, and serve rankings as one matrix multiply. item_tower.py

zero model calls at serving time
